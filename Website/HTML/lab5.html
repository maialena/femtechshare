WEEK 5: Layout
OVERVIEW

We're going to talk about perhaps the most important concept in web design and development: LAYOUT. A good layout is readable and highlights the most important information on the page. Here are 7 Pro Layout Tips to get you thinking about how you want to structure your page.

Today we'll take a closer look at something called 'responsive web design.' Responsive web design is the about making your content look good on any screen (tablet, phone, computer). There are many ways to do this, but today we'll go over three:

  1. Flexbox
  2. Bootstrap
  3. The Grid

Today we're jsut going to focus on Flexbox. Next class we'll dabble with Bootstrap, and if we have time, we'll venture into the Grid model (if not, it's left as an exercise to do on your own).

Creating a decent layout is perhaps the most confusing/frustrating parts of web development -- especially at the beginning. So it's important to spend adequate time building a strong foundation before jumping into more complex projects. If there are two things you take away from this course -- flexbox and bootstrap -- you'll be miles ahead of the game in the world of web development

Without further, ado, let's get started!

I. Flexbox
  • Pro's
    1. It's supported by most browsers
    2. It's dynamic, meaning that each flex items (ie the children of the flex container) will shrink or expand according to the size of your screen.
    3. Once you get the hang of it, it's easy to use.
  • Con's
    1. It may be difficult to learn at first, but this isn't really a Con because we're going to go through everything you need to know to get started together!

III (optional) Grid By Example

The Grid is magical.

  • Pro's
    1. It's supported by Chrome and a few other browsers (lookup)
    2. It's SUPER easy and convenient to use once you get the hang of it
    3. It makes your CSS Style sheet look beautiful and neat
  • Con's
    1. The biggest one: most browsers don't (currently) support the Grid. In fact, only IE officially does.

Yes, there's only one real Con -- but it's a biggie. If a Browser doesn't support the Grid, all your pretty formatting won't show up to the user, which is bad news bear. The good news is that a lot of browsers (Including Firefox, Chrome, and Safari) are in the process of developing support for the Grid since it's so 'up and coming.' In other words, it is still something valuable for you to learn. For the remainder of the lab, we'll work through the Grid examples on this website from CSS-Tricks

Create a new file in your Share folder (challenge: can you do this from your terminal?). Name it something like "grid_practice.html." Open it up and start walking through the exercises from Grid By Example above. If you don't understand something, ask for help or skip the exercise for now and come back.

Pro Tip: Try NOT to copy and paste the provided .html and .css files from the website. You'll learn more if you actually type it out.
